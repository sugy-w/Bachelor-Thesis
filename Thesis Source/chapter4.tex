\chapter{Next-day optimization of portfolio}

The goal of this chapter is to implement obtained prediction models and utilize their prediction ability
to construct an investment portfolio management framework. Within this chapter, an investment portfolio refers
to the allocating capital among particular stock assets in order to achieve various objectives (e.g., maximize return, minimize volatility, etc.).

\section{Markowitz mean-variance portfolio}

Allocating capital to a given set of stocks in order to balance the trade-off of asset volatility 
and portfolio return can be formulated as an optimization problem. There are several approaches
to properly constructing a portfolio strategy, each requiring different types of information from the data \cite{portfolio}.

For the purposes of this thesis, together with the obtained next-day price prediction of individual stocks, as
the most suitable framework can be considered \textbf{mean-variance portfolio} (MVP), introduced for the 
first time in 1952 by Harry Markowitz \cite{portfolio}.

Suppose $\mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^a$, where $a$ is the number of assets considered, is a vector of weights, and $\mathbf{w}_i$ represents
fraction of capital assigned to the $i$-th asset. Further, let $\bm{\Sigma} \in \mathbb{R}^{a \times a}$ denote the covariance matrix of assets price developments
in the given time interval. Lastly, let $\bm{\mu} \in \mathbb{R}^{a}$ represents the vector of expected returns of considered assets in the next time unit.

The goal is to balance the overall return of the portfolio (represented as $\mathbf{w}^{\top}\bm{\mu}$) with the portfolio variance (represented as $\mathbf{w}^{\top}\bm{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}$). Such 
a bi-objective optimization problem can be formulated as follows:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:4.1}
    \max_{\mathbf{w}} \>\>\> \mathbf{w}^{\top}\bm{\mu} - \lambda \mathbf{w}^{\top}\bm{\Sigma}\mathbf{w},
\end{equation}
where $\lambda$ is a hyperparameter controlling the investor's aversion to the risk. This problem aligns with the assumption that higher returns yield higher risk \cite{portfolio}.

For the purposes of this thesis, it is sufficient to introduce certain constraints in Equation \eqref{eq:4.1}. In the considered strategy, short selling of stocks (seeking profit from an asset's decline) is forbidden
(represented as $\mathbf{w} \geq \mathbf{0}_a$). Lastly, we require that the entire capital be allocated, with no uninvested funds (represented as $\mathbf{1}_a^{\top}\mathbf{w}=1$).

However, framework in Equation \eqref{eq:4.1}, together with the introduced constraints, tends to be impractical in real-world scenarios \cite{portfolio}. The problem arises with frequent re-allocations
and transaction costs in practice. Particularly, frequent re-allocations generate higher costs for the investor and subsequently lead to a decline in profit. With next-day predictions
considered in this thesis, we decided to maintain the daily re-allocations; however, to minimize day-to-day transactions, we introduced an ad hoc modification of the framework based on Equation \eqref{eq:4.1}.

Consider the variable $\mathbf{w}_t \in \mathbb{R}^a$ representing the portfolio allocation weights on day $t$ and $\bm{\mu}_t \in \mathbb{R}^a$ being the expected returns 
of the assets on day $t$. Taking into account Equation \eqref{eq:4.1} and the objective of minimizing transactions, it is possible to define the optimization problem of capital allocation on day $t$ as follows:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\max_{\mathbf{w}_t} \quad 
& \mathbf{w}_t^{\top}\bm{\mu}_t 
- \lambda_{\text{R}} \mathbf{w}_t^{\top}\bm{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}_t 
- \lambda_{T} \|\mathbf{w}_t - \mathbf{w}_{t-1}\|_1 \\
\\
\text{Subject to:} \quad 
& \mathbf{w}_t \geq \mathbf{0}_a \>\>\> \text{and} \>\>\> \mathbf{1}_a^{\top}\mathbf{w}=1
\label{eq:4.2}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_R$ denotes risk-aversion hyperparameter and $\lambda_T$ stands for approximate cost of a single transaction \cite{portfolio}. This modified MVP framework (with constant $\lambda_T = 0.01$) will be used in all analyses presented in the chapter.

\section{Portfolio evaluation metrics}

In order to evaluate success and to compare different portfolio-building settings, it
is sufficient to define comparative metrics that measure specific qualities of a portfolio.

Suppose strategy is applied over a time interval of length $N$. We can track the value of the portfolio over this interval; let
the portfolio values be represented as a discrete time series $V = V_1, V_2, \ldots, V_N$.

To measure the average percentage return per year, we can define:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:4.3}
    \text{CAGR}(V) = \left(\frac{V_N}{V_1}\right)^ {\frac{1}{r}} - 1 ,
\end{equation}
where $r$ is the number of trading years between $V_1$ and $V_N$. The metric in Equation \eqref{eq:4.3} represents the \textbf{cumulative annual growth return} (CAGR) \cite{portfolio}.

In order to quantify volatility of the strategy, let $r_t = \frac{V_t}{V_{t-1}} - 1$ denote the return on day $t$. Volatility over a given
period is given as a sample standard deviation of $r_1, r_2, \ldots, r_N$ defined as $\sigma = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N}\sum_{t=1}^N (r_t - \overline{r})^2}$, where $\overline{r}$ denotes
the sample mean of $r_1, r_2, \ldots, r_N$. To annualize this metric, we define:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:4.4}
    \text{AV}(V) = \sigma \sqrt{P},
\end{equation}
where $P$ is the number of periods (trading days) per year. This metric is referred to as \textbf{annualized volatility} (AV) \cite{portfolio}.

Last but not least, it is possible to define a compound metric that takes into account both Equations \eqref{eq:4.3} and \eqref{eq:4.4}. Let us define:
\begin{equation}
    \text{Sharpe ratio}(V) = \frac{\text{CAGR}(V)}{\text{AV}(V)},
\end{equation}
which is referred to as the \textbf{Sharpe ratio} and represents the return the strategy earns per unit of risk \cite{portfolio}.

\section{Evaluation on the training-validation interval}

Despite the total number of considered variants of models being $8$, we decided to include subset of them in the portfolio-building analyses. Naturally, we chose ARIMA model, as its dominance was the highest among 
all the models. Further, we decided to include both Ridge and Lasso  (in their General model form). These two were selected to analyze the performance of two marginally different model frameworks (in term of both MAPE and model dominance).

In all the subsequent analyses, the main focus will be on tuning of $\lambda_R$, the risk-aversion hyperparameter.

\subsection{Naïve portfolio}
As a benchmark portfolio we chose to employ introduced naïve benchmark prediction model (see Section \ref{naive}). Effectively, this framework is based on the a priori assumption that,
in optimization problem introduced in Equation \eqref{eq:4.2}, term $\bm{\mu}_t \in \mathbb{R}^a$ representing expected returns satisfies $\bm{\mu}_t = \mathbf{0}_a$ – expecting no returns in the following day, indirectly
employing naïve model as the prediction engine.

In this case, it is suitable to analyze the performance of the set-up portfolio with various values of risk-aversion parameter $\lambda_R$. Dependence of CAGR on the chosen value of $\lambda_R$ is depicted in Figure \ref{fig:4.1}.

It is possible to observe that the CAGR converges to a value of approximately $22.5 \%$ when $\lambda_R > 500$. However, the optimal value is observed at $\lambda_R = 300$, where the overall CAGR exceeds $23 \%$.
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{images/naive_cagr.png}
    \caption{\centering Dependence of CAGR on chosen $\lambda_R$ value (Naïve model).}
    \label{fig:4.1}
\end{figure}

The dependence of AV on $\lambda_R$ is depicted in Figure \ref{fig:4.2}. In this case a clear convergence is observed as $\lambda_R$ increases. Moreover, the decline in AV for $\lambda_R > 1000$ remains negligible. This yields quite a few possible management decisions. Either we focus on keeping the CAGR highest possible (even though with not optimal AV) or
we try to balance these two metrics to achieve approximately optimal results both in volatility and return. 
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{images/naive_av.png}
    \caption{\centering Dependence of AV on chosen $\lambda_R$ value (Naïve model).}
    \label{fig:4.2}
\end{figure}
In this case, we decided to balance volatility and potential return of the portfolio by setting $\lambda_R = 1000$. Evaluation of this portfolio is presented in Table \ref{tab:naive_portoflio}, together with evaluations of conventional indices available for investors.

A marginal difference can be observed, with naïve portfolio clearly outperforming both indices in all the evaluation metrics.
Such result, obtained by the naïve portfolio, is quite optimistic, even when taking into account transaction costs. This fact might root in 
segment focus of the portfolio on IT segment, which had experienced major surge in the recent years.

\subsection{Lasso portfolio}
In Figure \ref{fig:4.4}, the dependence of CAGR on the chosen value of $\lambda_R$ for Lasso model is depicted. Similar to the case shown in Figure \ref{fig:4.1}, it is possible to observe direct proportion between CAGR and $\lambda_R$.

Further, dependence of AV on chosen value of $\lambda_R$ is visualized in Figure \ref{fig:4.5}. 
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{images/lasso_av.png}
    \caption{\centering Dependence of AV on chosen $\lambda_R$ value (Lasso model).}
    \label{fig:4.5}

In the case of managing volatility, this fully aligns with the purpose of the $\lambda_R$ in portfolio optimization. By making portfolio more conservative, overall volatility and risk decrease. Interestingly, the AV appears
to convergence to approximately $17 \%$. By increasing the value of $\lambda_R$ over $1000$, decline in AV is negligible.

By increasing the value of $\lambda_R$ beyond $2000$, gains in CAGR were minimal, ont the order of $10^{-2} - 10^{-1} \%$. Taking into account, that further increasing does not necessarily improve risk-management, we chose to set $\lambda_R = 2000$. 

The resulting portfolio has a CAGR $\mathbf{20.15 \%}$, an AV $\mathbf{16.8 \%}$ and a Sharpe ratio $\mathbf{1.2}$. On average, capital was allocated to $\mathbf{11.1}$ stock assets per day (out of total $65$).
The Lasso portfolio was unable to outperform naïve in terms of CAGR; however, in terms of volatility, it is approximately $0.2 \%$ lower in absolute manner.
